Building trust in the AI era with privacy-led UX

A significant shift is underway in the way companies approach user consent, with a growing focus on "privacy-led UX" that treats transparency around data collection and usage as a key aspect of the customer relationship. According to Adelina Peltea, CMO at Usercentrics, this approach is no longer seen as a trade-off between growth and compliance, but rather as a driver of business growth, with well-designed consent experiences outperforming initial estimates. By introducing data-sharing decisions gradually, companies can gather higher-quality consumer data, which can compound in value over time. This approach is particularly crucial as AI systems become more prevalent, as clear privacy and data transparency policies are essential for responsible AI deployment. However, realizing the advantages of privacy-led UX requires cross-functional collaboration and clear leadership, with CMOs often best positioned to own the strategy. As AI introduces new levels of complexity, companies must adapt their consent infrastructure to go beyond traditional cookie banners, with a practical framework for evaluating and improving privacy-led UX essential for consistency at every consent touchpoint. What remains uncertain is how companies will balance the need for transparency with the increasing complexity of AI systems, and how regulatory frameworks will evolve to address these challenges.

tech The Verge

FTC pushes ad agencies into dropping brand safety rules

The FTC's proposed settlement with major ad agencies, including WPP, Publicis, and Dentsu, marks a significant shift in the regulation of online advertising, as it prohibits these agencies from collectively boycotting platforms based on their political viewpoints. This move is notable, as it comes on the heels of a judge dismissing a similar lawsuit from X, which had alleged that ad agencies were engaging in an "illegal boycott" by avoiding platforms with certain types of content. The FTC's complaint highlights the role of organizations like NewsGuard and the Global Disinformation Index in classifying certain opinions as "misinformation" and lobbying ad buyers to demonetize sites hosting such content, with 75% of online advertisers relying on these third-party ratings to inform their ad buys. The proposed order, which requires the ad agencies to file annual compliance reports and appoint a compliance monitor, is expected to have far-reaching consequences for the digital advertising ecosystem, potentially leading to increased ad revenue for platforms that were previously avoided. However, the settlement also raises questions about the balance between brand safety and free speech, with some arguing that it could lead to the proliferation of misinformation online. What remains uncertain is how the ad agencies will adapt to the new regulations and whether the settlement will ultimately achieve the FTC's goal of restoring competition to the digital news ecosystem.

Import AI 445: Timing superintelligence; AIs solve frontier math proofs; a new ML research benchmark

The pursuit of superintelligence may be a calculated risk worth taking, as it could lead to significant improvements in human health and lifespan, outweighing the potential risks. According to Nick Bostrom, developing superintelligence could be favorable even with a non-zero chance of doom, as the benefits of extending human life expectancy and improving quality of life could be substantial. For instance, Bostrom notes that "our individual life expectancy is higher if superintelligence is developed reasonably soon," and that "the life we stand to gain would plausibly be of immensely higher quality than the life we risk forfeiting." This perspective challenges the common assumption that slowing down or pausing AI development is the safest approach, instead suggesting that a brief pause may only be effective right at the end of the exponential growth curve. Meanwhile, advancements in AI research are accelerating, with Facebook's development of Kunlun, a more efficient recommendation system, demonstrating the optimization of societally significant AI systems. Kunlun's improved Model FLOPs Utilization (MFU) and predictable scaling laws could enable more effective investments in AI development, potentially leading to better performance and more targeted advertising. Additionally, the introduction of the AI Research Science Benchmark (AIRS-BENCH) shows that AI agents can independently complete basic AI research tasks, such as code generation and time series forecasting, although the current models tested are not yet state-of-the-art. As AI capabilities continue to improve, we can expect to see more significant breakthroughs in areas like human-AI collaboration and AI safety research. The key variables in determining the optimal timing for superintelligence development are the risk of a superintelligence killing us all and the rate at which safety research can reduce this chance. Bostrom's research suggests that developing superintelligence becomes a favorable thing to do under most circumstances, with the speed of progress and maturity of AI safety research having some impact on the timeline. However, critics of this view might argue that it's akin to trying to catch a falling knife, where timing is everything, and a brief pause may only be effective right at the end. Ultimately, the pursuit of superintelligence requires careful consideration of the potential risks and benefits, as well as continued advancements in AI safety research. In terms of numbers, Kunlun's improved MFU has resulted in a 1.2% improvement in topline metrics, while the AIRS-BENCH has tested out 20 distinct tasks sourced from 17 recent machine learning papers. The development of superintelligence could lead to significant improvements in human life expectancy, with Bostrom noting that "the choice before us is not between a risk-free baseline and a risky AI venture, but between different risky trajectories, each exposing us to a different set of hazards." As AI research continues to advance, it's essential to consider the potential implications of superintelligence and the importance of careful planning and development. The potential benefits of superintelligence, such as improved human health and lifespan, must be weighed against the potential risks, including the risk of a superintelligence killing us all. However, with careful consideration of the key variables and continued advancements in AI safety research, the pursuit of superintelligence may be a calculated risk worth taking. As Bostrom notes, "swift to harbor, slow to berth: move quickly towards AGI capability, and then, as we gain more information about the remaining safety challenges and specifics of the situation, be prepared to possibly slow down and make adjustments as we navigate the critical stages of scaleup and deployment."
